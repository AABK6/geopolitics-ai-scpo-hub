Frontier AI is not just software. It is an industrial pipeline built on rival inputs: compute, energy, data, talent, and the institutions that can coordinate them at scale.

01 1.0 Paradigm Shift

From Software to Sovereignty

Frontier AI does not behave like weightless software. Strategically, what matters is the industrial system that turns code into usable power.

Early scaling narratives treated models as if they were naturally diffusive: copyable, downloadable, and therefore difficult to territorialize. That intuition captures the non-rival character of ideas, but it misses the infrastructure that makes frontier capability real.

Training runs, inference fleets, cooling systems, curated datasets, power contracts, and operating teams all reintroduce rival constraints. AI therefore pulls geopolitics back toward an older grammar of industrial power: who owns the inputs, who controls the chokepoints, and who can deny scale to others.

The question is no longer only which lab writes the best model architecture. It is which state-firm assemblage can mobilize the full production system behind that architecture.

The shift in what matters

Capability does not emerge from code alone. It emerges when rival inputs, institutions, and delivery systems align in the same place.

Sovereignty returns where states and firms can govern access, cost, siting, and delivery across that full pipeline.

Non-rival layer
Weights, architectures, and software logic can still spread faster than steel mills or oil fields.
Rival layer
Compute clusters, electricity, data rights, cooling systems, and skilled operators remain scarce, located, and governed.
Strategic effect
The decisive advantage shifts from raw model novelty to the ability to assemble and sustain the whole industrial pipeline.

02 1.1 Production Function

The AI Production Function

At scale, AI capability depends on linked inputs rather than code alone. Once those inputs are named, the political stakes become easier to see.

Core Concept
A = f(C,E,D) ×T×η

AI capability at scale is produced by compute, energy, and data, then multiplied by scarce talent and by the efficiency gains that software can deliver.

Input C C

Compute

Chips, clusters, networking, and datacenter integration.

Input E E

Energy

Electricity, grid capacity, cooling, and site readiness.

Input D D

Data

Access, rights, freshness, domain coverage, and industrial telemetry.

Multiplier T T

Talent

Researchers, systems engineers, and operators who can deploy at scale.

Multiplier η η

Efficiency

Algorithmic and software advances that convert inputs into capability more effectively.

What the formula changes

Constraint logic
These inputs are substitutes only at the margin. Efficiency can relieve compute pressure, but at frontier scale the bottlenecks remain complementary rather than cleanly replaceable.
Political logic
If capability depends on rival inputs, sovereignty returns through supply chains, infrastructure, and the institutions that can mobilize them.
State response
Tariffs, subsidies, export controls, standards, procurement, immigration policy, and permitting all matter again.
Strategic effect
Capability reconcentrates around actors that can coordinate capital expenditure, infrastructure access, labor, and organizational scale.
03 1.2 Compute

Compute as Capital Moat and Chokepoint

Compute is both the most visible frontier input and the clearest example of how industrial concentration shapes AI power.

Frontier training runs now require clusters measured in tens of thousands of accelerators and expenditures measured in the hundreds of millions. That immediately narrows the field to actors with unusual access to capital and supply.

The stack is concentrated at multiple layers. NVIDIA dominates frontier accelerators, TSMC remains central to advanced fabrication, and memory, networking, and datacenter engineering add further bottlenecks to the path from chip design to usable capability.

The strategic result is a dual logic: compute is a capital moat for frontier labs and a policy chokepoint for states that can regulate access to chips, tools, and cloud pathways.

Scale

Frontier clusters now run from roughly 10,000 to 100,000 GPUs at the high end of current training scale.

Concentration

NVIDIA design and TSMC fabrication concentrate leverage at the top of the stack.

Cost

Training frontier-scale systems can require roughly $100M-$500M runs, before inference buildout.

The frontier system stack

AI accelerators

Frontier capability starts with a narrow accelerator market dominated by a small number of suppliers.

Advanced fabrication

Cutting-edge logic manufacturing remains geographically concentrated and difficult to replicate quickly.

Memory and interconnect

HBM and high-bandwidth networking determine whether chips can operate as a coherent training cluster.

Datacenter engineering

Cooling, power delivery, and cluster integration turn components into reliable frontier-scale compute.

2026 policy update

Deny + monetize

Old frame
Export controls were read mainly as denial: block access to the chips that matter most.
Update
The January 2026 White House proclamation and BIS rule made some diffusion conditional, priced, and case-by-case rather than simply banned.
Effect
Compute policy becomes a mix of restriction, certification, and monetized diffusion instead of a single denial story.

Chokepoint geometry

The point is not one monopoly alone. It is the sequence of narrow gates that makes compute simultaneously a capital moat and a policy lever.

04 1.3 Energy

Energy Becomes the Binding Constraint

Once compute is secured, the next bottleneck is often not another chip but the electricity, grid access, and permitting needed to activate the cluster.

Energy now sits at the center of the production function because AI deployment is becoming power intensive at system scale. IEA projects electricity demand from AI and data centers rising sharply through 2030, while EIA ties the strongest recent U.S. electricity-demand growth directly to data centers.

This is where the idea of stranded intelligence becomes useful. A lab can have capital, chips, and ambition, yet still fail to scale if the grid cannot deliver reliable baseload power fast enough.

The political consequence is that power delivery, co-location, transmission, and rate allocation stop looking like background infrastructure. They become part of AI strategy itself.

Scarcity before response

Demand pressure

IEA treats AI and data centers as a fast-rising electricity-demand segment, and EIA links recent U.S. demand growth directly to datacenter expansion.

Grid friction

Connection queues, co-location disputes, and reliability concerns shift AI expansion into the terrain of grid governance and public regulation.

Firm-power response

Hyperscalers and their partners increasingly seek always-on supply through nuclear restarts, advanced reactor partnerships, and long-horizon power agreements.

Constraint to response

Power becomes strategy
Demand

AI load growth

Training and inference push datacenter demand into long-horizon planning.

Constraint

Grid congestion

Queues, co-location fights, and transmission limits create stranded intelligence.

Response

Firm-power deals

Hyperscalers seek nuclear, advanced reactors, and regulatory accommodation.

The sequence matters: scarcity arrives first, and only then does firm power appear as the strategic response.

Illustrative responses

Microsoft / Constellation

The Crane restart is framed as a way to put carbon-free baseload power back onto the grid for a power-hungry digital economy.

Google / Kairos / TVA

Kairos ties advanced reactor deployment directly to load growth, with 500 MW of capacity targeted by 2035.

Meta

Meta frames nuclear projects as a route to reliable firm power for American AI leadership, at a scale reaching up to 6.6 GW by 2035.

FERC

Co-location disputes show that datacenter power access is now a regulated conflict over reliability and cost allocation, not a private back-office detail.

04A 1.3a Water

Water as Co-Binding Constraint

Water is a newer but increasingly important scaling condition: smaller in global share than electricity, but sharply concentrated where facilities actually sit.

Water matters because cooling load is local. A hyperscale campus does not compete against all freshwater use on earth; it competes inside a specific basin, permitting regime, and municipal infrastructure system.

That is why the strategic issue is not planetary spectacle but siting politics. In water-stressed regions, a large datacenter can trigger fights over cooling methods, recycling systems, and who bears the local burden of AI expansion.

The result is a new marginal advantage for locations that can pair firm power with water resilience. Cooling technology, wastewater reuse, and basin stress start to matter alongside price and latency.

What water changes

Cooling load
Water intensity varies by cooling architecture, climate, and facility design rather than by one universal benchmark.
Local politics
Permitting fights emerge where datacenter demand intersects with stressed basins, agricultural use, or urban growth.
Operational response
Closed-loop systems, different cooling strategies, and water-recycling partnerships become part of the infrastructure strategy.
Geopolitical effect
Water-rich sites with reliable power gain a measurable advantage in marginal scaling capacity.
05 1.4 Data

Data Moves from Commons to Governed Asset

Three dynamics explain why training material becomes strategic: monetization and gatekeeping, strategic localization, and quality scarcity.

Easy web-scale scraping was always a transitional condition, not a permanent settlement. As frontier systems exhaust cheap public text and shift toward higher-quality reasoning corpora, data rights become harder to separate from market power and institutional control.

Platforms, publishers, and states now compete over who can gate, license, localize, and legally define the data that matters most for training. The result is not the disappearance of data flows, but the end of treating them as politically neutral.

Dynamic 1

Monetization and gatekeeping

Examples on the page: OpenAI’s Reddit partnership and X’s own privacy-policy clarification about AI model training.
Dynamic 2

Strategic localization

States increasingly treat public-service, health, education, and defense-relevant datasets as sovereignty resources rather than frictionless inputs.

The strategic question shifts from access alone to jurisdiction, permissible transfer, and who governs downstream use.
Dynamic 3

Quality scarcity and industrial data

High-quality human data, proprietary feedback loops, and industrial telemetry gain value as generic web text saturates.

This is where the argument moves from generic scraping toward reasoning data, RLHF-style feedback, and the industrial web.
Legal pressure

The New York Times complaint does not settle the legal question, but it shows that training-data access now sits inside copyright conflict and legal risk.

Production and hosting are not the same thing

China produces top researchers 47%
United States hosts top researchers 57%

MacroPolo’s tracker shows the asymmetry clearly: one country produces a large share of frontier talent, another remains the leading destination.

Capture

Open systems attract globally mobile researchers and engineers, which is why immigration and research ecosystems remain strategic assets.

Control

Securitization, loyalty tests, and restrictive controls can protect against leakage, but they also risk degrading the openness that makes the system attractive.

06 1.5 Talent

Talent Remains the Human Bottleneck

Talent is the paradoxical input: the scarcest in the short run and the most globally mobile at the same time.

Elite AI researchers and systems engineers are not produced on demand. Frontier talent is a long-cycle input that can take more than a decade to develop, which makes it resistant to quick policy fixes.

That scarcity interacts with global mobility. States want the gains from openness when they are trying to attract frontier talent, but they also want tighter control when AI becomes securitized.

The result is a sovereignty dilemma rather than a simple race: how much openness can a system retain while still treating frontier AI as a strategic domain?

07 1.6 Efficiency

Efficiency as the Counter-Force

Efficiency matters because it destabilizes any simple story in which hardware denial automatically settles the balance of power.

If capability can improve with less compute and less energy, export controls do not end the competition. They also create adaptation pressure: constrained actors have a stronger reason to innovate on software, model design, and training recipes.

DeepSeek is the clearest proof of concept for that counter-strategy. The point is not that efficiency abolishes infrastructure, but that it can erode the leverage of purely brute-force denial.

Mixture-of-Experts

Large parameter counts no longer imply that every parameter is activated on every token, reducing cost per inference and per training step.

Distillation

Capabilities learned by large models can be compressed into smaller systems, widening diffusion below the absolute frontier.

Training-stack optimization

Data quality filtering, better load balancing, and architectural tuning can substitute for brute-force scale only up to a point, but they matter.

Proof of Concept

DeepSeek as adaptation pressure

DeepSeek-V3 and R1 show how software and training efficiency can become a geopolitical counter-strategy. The lesson is not that compute stops mattering, but that constrained hardware environments can still produce meaningful capability gains.

Claimed direct cost $5.6M

DeepSeek-V3 is useful because it shows how efficient design can narrow the gap to much larger training budgets.

Frontier comparison $100M-$500M

U.S. frontier training runs still define the high-capital benchmark that efficiency is trying to offset rather than erase.

Unknown 1

If efficiency compounds

Chokepoint leverage erodes over time because denial keeps generating adaptation incentives and software spreads more easily than fabs or grids.

Unknown 2

If efficiency hits ceilings

Compute concentration retains its force because the frontier remains stubbornly capital intensive and physically bottlenecked.

08 1.7 Institutions & Capital

Institutions and Capital Bind the Stack Together

The final move in Part 1 is not a flourish. It explains why only a narrow set of actors can actually turn all the previous inputs into frontier capability.

Frontier AI is no longer a domain where a small team can scale on code quality alone. It now requires capital expenditure, cloud access, long-cycle infrastructure decisions, and the institutional ability to align regulators, suppliers, engineers, and financing.

That is why frontier AI is an organizational feat as much as a scientific one. States cannot simply declare AI power into existence; they must build the enabling industrial system or bargain with the firms that already command it.

What only a few actors can assemble

Capital
Frontier runs require balance sheets that can absorb datacenter buildout, long procurement timelines, and uncertain commercialization paths.
Cloud
Hyperscaler infrastructure remains a central delivery mechanism for training, inference, and enterprise deployment.
Energy
Power contracts and grid access decide whether compute can move from procurement to operation.
Policy
Export controls, procurement, liability, and permitting shape who can assemble the frontier stack and where.
Operations
Integration capacity turns isolated inputs into a functioning industrial system rather than a pile of expensive bottlenecks.
